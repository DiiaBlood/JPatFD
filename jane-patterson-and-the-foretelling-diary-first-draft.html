Chapter 1: The Foretelling Diary

It was happening again.

Jane Patterson opened her eyes.

And what she saw was nothing but nothingness itself. Thick darkness veiled the surroundings from all directions, and only one entity stood center stage: a gleaming, red bathtub. She felt her chest shiver—the slightly painful type of shiver.

"H-hello…? Is… is anyone here?" she inquired, turning her head away from the tub and checking for any possible reaction. "I'm scared…" Her statement was only met by its own echo. Though the scenery wasn't unfamiliar, It still gave Jane the heebie-jeebies. It wasn't her fault the only dream she had was this abomination.

Standing in the void amidst deafening silence was something, but being in the same space as that bathtub, was something else entirely. It stank with ominosity, as if knives and hammers swung all round it, or as if touching it would poison her to death, or simply hurt her. The fear was as much instinctive as it was reactional, yet, it wasn't logical, as she had no real reason to fear the tub. With no desire to walk up to it, Jane decided to walk away from it. She moved a good five-meters and turned back. The bathtub was as close as it was before she had moved. Of course, she thought. Nothing new.

But suddenly, an idea struck her. Jane was a tiny kid, not that she liked that fact, and due to her height, she was never able to see what was in the bathtub from the distance it was in before. Coming closer to it was an idea out of the picture; out of the room; and out the house, but peeking at what was inside of it intrigued her. She took a long breath and raised her body atop her tippy-toes. Right at the distant end of the bathtub, she saw what looked to be deep, blue water, and the moment that water reflected in Jane's eyes, something squeezed her heart like a stress toy. Suddenly, there was no floor, and Jane was falling at high speed.

It was only then, when she truly opened her eye.

Jane jolted awake, her heart racing. The left side of her chin and the shoulder below it were completely damp in drool, likewise the open book in her lap. One of the lines was drenched, but barely readable: Big Bee wants justice. Big Bee wants honey.

She looked around in a daze, seeing towers of bookshelves and orange stars made of glass. Did I sleep the entire night? A glimpse at the clock above the broad double-doors showed that the small, chunky hand pointed at IV and the tall, thin one between XI and XII. She pondered for a second as her brain ran calculations. Six… no, no… When there is an I to the left, you take one away… so four in the morning. There was plenty of time before the bell rang and the day began.

"Morning there, missy," an old man said, causing Jane to jump and fall on her side. The man croaked a chuckle and bent over his round desk, smiling to her. "You snored like a hummingbird," he said, his gentle eyes unassuming above his round spectacles.

"G-good morning… to… too, Mr. Angelo," she said, her mind attempting to imagine what a hummingbird was before she could imagine it snoring. Mr. Angelo drew back and disappeared from Jane's vision. It was best if she went back to her reading, but she wasn't that delighted to go through the book for the umpteenth time. She thought for a moment, and realized a possible solution, though it required her to use her voice again… She shivered, but stood up nevertheless, wiping away the drool off her face. She left her book at the floor, making a mental note to return to it later.

"Um, Mr. Angelo…" she mumbled, only the top half of her pale, ghostly face, half-obscured by black hair, visible above the desktop, "Is… is there… is there…" She was taken back by the fact that Mr. Angelo was reading his book upside down. "Wh-why… read it that way?"

He looked at the book, back at Jane, then smiled, "Why not?"

Mr. Angelo was always strange, Jane reminded herself. Back on topic, "I was… gonna ask. Is there anything you recommend?"

"Of course," he said with enthusiastic body language, "everything!"

"How about… um… something more specific?" Jane was not about read everything, unless that was a book on its own. She looked down for a moment, her pink slippers looked back at her with googly eyes. "Something… nice?" she said, looking up cautiously to check Mr. Angelo's expression. His optics drilled into her with a thoughtful look. She looked away again. "Y-you know… Like all the other ones I read."

He sighed, "Unfortunately, you've read all those books." Jane heard noises of thumps and papers moving. "But how about this." She looked up and found Mr. Angelo holding up a book with the title The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes written above, and By A. Conan Doyle written below it. She knew this one. "It has… murder in it," she said.

"I do not… like it…"

"It's a little difficult, sure, but—"

"I don't want to."

"You haven't given it a try. If only you—"

"No!" she said in Mr. Angelo's face with loud enough volume to leave a trail of reverberation in the room. Upon realizing her ill-manners, Jane covered her mouth and looked down in shame. "I'm sorry," she whimpered.

"You'll eventually run out, you know," Mr. Angelo said with a warm, but firm, voice. "You'll eventually be left with nothing but the books you hate, and what will you do then?"

Jane stayed silent at that. Part of her regretted asking Mr. Angelo in the first place.

Mr. Angelo took a deep inhale, then a long exhale. From the corner of her watery eye, Jane saw his hand rise and point in a direction behind her. "You'll find a new batch of books below Saint Mathew's window. Maybe something there will catch your attention."

Jane lifted her head slowly. Mr. Angelo returned to his usual smile, but she couldn't help but feel that there was a hint of trouble in his eyes. It was probably her that troubled him. At the very least, she would need to keep her manners in place.

"Thank you," she said with a nod, and he nodded too, before returning to his upside down study.

"You okay?" Alice tried patting Jane on the head.

"I'm fine… b-but… why didn't you tell her about him k-kicking you down the stairs…?"

Alice shrugged. "I think he actually didn't mean it."

The end of Jane's lips rose uncontrollably until she laughed quietly.

"What are you laughing about, ey?" Alice poked her in the ribs with her crutch, which caused her to laugh even more.

"N-n-nothing…!" she walked away from the reach of the crutch.

Jane was truly inspired by Alice.

"Anyhoo…" Alice grabbed onto her black skirt with an uncomfortable expression, "kinda need to pee… badly…"

"Uh," Jane tilted her head, "good luck?"

Alice held a laugh as she hobbled away. "You're funny."

Feeling that she needed to sit, Jane walked back to the church again. She felt as light as a feather as she took a seat, her breathing cold, and her mind calm. It all, for once, was good.

But it was then that a thought appeared in her mind.

At the church today, I met—

Jane suddenly felt the presence of a towering shadow sitting next to her—where her eye saw no light. Her heart froze, and it was almost as if death itself had embraced her from the back, its scythe swinging near her neck.

"So, child," the shadow began speaking with a voice so threateningly calm, "what knowledge has Father John bestowed upon you today?"

Jane's breathing was broken. She inhaled and exhaled, but her brain kept clogging up. Something about this was wrong—very wrong.

"I hear of him going with sin as the subject of today. I am intrigued," every move from the shadow was only audible through rustles of clothes, and each move stabbed Jane in the heart. "Jesus has died for us. He and his sacrifice purified our sins. What a joke." Jane winced as he spat the last words. "Say, child, what do you say?"

Jane's eyes began leaking, but she forced herself not to make a single noise out of it. Out of sheer force, she had said what she thought first.

"S-sin… is th-the wrong we… m-m-must right…"

There was a quick exhale from the shadow. It was surely a chuckle or a snort of sorts, but its tone felt unnatural.

"That has Mary Patterson written all over it. Say, child, it is your turn to ask me what I think. It's only fair."

Like a marionette, her lips opened. "Wh-what d-d-do you th-think…?"

"Glad you asked," he put a hand around Jane, and she could only wish to be in her grave by that point, "I say to erase it." He came closer and whispered. "Who would notice a small grain of sand taken from an hourglass? It's a crime with no thief."

Jane shut her eyes close, her mind closed, and expected that scythe to slice her neck.

"Father Oswald! What an honor to see you here!" said the familiar, glad Father John.

Feeling like she's no longer alone with this shadow, Jane gathered the courage to look up and see. And see, she did. It was no death or shadow, but a simple-looking bold man. He wore a black rope and had a silver cross dangling from his neck. His brows—or lack there of—were raised in serendipity. He closed his deep dark eyes and laughed.

"Was just messin' around," he ruffled Jane's hair and brought up a wrapped plank. "A friend brought me this, and for a hot moment, I thought I could keep it. But I couldn't help but feel guilty for stealing from the artist." He winked at Jane with a mischievous grin.

He gave the plank to Jane, and she tore its wrapping apart after wiping her tears, curious to see that… it was just her painting of herself from a couple days ago. Her ugly drawing of a vase with two eyes, one of which had Xs and scratches covering it.

"You l-liked this…?" Jane asked.

"It's beautiful," Father Oswald said before standing up.

Jane's internal warning systems all began returning to normal, but she couldn't help but to think why they all activated to begin with. It's just like the diary and Alice! I have to stop being so pessimistic!

The man bowed lightly to the pastor of the church and said, "Father John."

Father John bowed in return, "Father Oswald."

I feel like I know this name… Deep within the vines of her mind, something pulsated with that name, but it was obvious.

Father Oswald still refuses to change his mind about the church donations!

He was the man, with his charming smile and his playful attitude.

He was the one who wanted the orphanage down.
